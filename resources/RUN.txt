Final Thoughts:
We we have seen, choosing what cities to avoid can have substantial impact on our route selection.
When there is no restriction, we have the maximum flexibility to generate the shortest route possible.
However, adding cities to avoid, especially multiple cities, can substantially increase the length of the route.
The best case to show this is when we choose to avoid all flights out of Seattle, essentially stranding the city and
depriving it of any option to travel to the destination city. However, if the city we choose to avoid was never part of
the shortest route to begin with, then it won't have any effect at all. How the dijsktra algorithm behaves to implement
this feature is interesting as well. As we can see from the results, if we choose to avoid a city, the distance between
the starting city and that city will be infinity, simplifying that it is simply unreachable. Lastly, we have seen in
some cases that the shortest route to a further city will incorporate the shortest route to a closer city, but this is
not always the case. While there can certainly be overlaps, it can also be the case that there is a shorter route
between the starting city and further destination by going through a third city, as opposed to following the shortest
route to a closer city.
